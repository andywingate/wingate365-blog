<!--IMAGE: Overview of brewing setup with kettle on cart and equipment (IMG_9981.jpeg or IMG_9971.jpeg)-->
<p>The main vessel was filled with 80 L of water and brought up to 61°C.
While we waited for the water to heat, the malt was ground. The grain bill for this batch was:</p>
<p><strong>Malt Bill:</strong>&nbsp;Pilsner: 5.22 kg, Wiener: 11.48 kg, Münchner: 7.83 kg, Caramel: 1.57 kg</p>
<!--IMAGE: Recipe sheet on wall showing malt bill and schedule (IMG_9975.jpeg or IMG_9987.jpeg)-->
<p>Once the water reached 61°C the malt was added and the mash left to rest for 10 mins. The temperature was then raised to 63°C before resting again for 45 min. Followed by another period of heating, this time to 72°C and a further rest of 45 min.&nbsp;</p><h3 style="text-align: left;">Why is the Mashing Process multiple steps?</h3><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]"><strong>The Basic Goal:</strong> Extract fermentable sugars from the grain, but the grain contains complex starches that need to be broken down by enzymes naturally present in the malt.</p><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]"><strong>Why Different Temperatures?</strong></p><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">Different enzymes work best at different temperatures, and they produce different results:</p><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]"><strong>61°C - Initial Rest (10 minutes)</strong></p><ul class="[li_&amp;]:mb-0 [li_&amp;]:mt-1.5 [li_&amp;]:gap-1.5 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ul]:pb-1 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ol]:pb-1 list-disc flex flex-col gap-2 pl-8 mb-3">
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">This is the <strong>protein rest</strong> zone</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">Breaks down proteins in the malt</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">Improves clarity and head retention in the final beer</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">Creates free amino nitrogen that yeast will need during fermentation</li>
</ul><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]"><strong>63°C - First Main Rest (45 minutes)</strong></p><ul class="[li_&amp;]:mb-0 [li_&amp;]:mt-1.5 [li_&amp;]:gap-1.5 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ul]:pb-1 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ol]:pb-1 list-disc flex flex-col gap-2 pl-8 mb-3">
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">This activates <strong>beta-amylase enzymes</strong></li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">These enzymes create more fermentable sugars (maltose)</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">Results in a drier beer with higher alcohol potential</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">Works slowly but creates sugars that yeast can easily consume</li>
</ul><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]"><strong>72°C - Second Main Rest (45 minutes)</strong></p><ul class="[li_&amp;]:mb-0 [li_&amp;]:mt-1.5 [li_&amp;]:gap-1.5 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ul]:pb-1 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ol]:pb-1 list-disc flex flex-col gap-2 pl-8 mb-3">
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">This activates <strong>alpha-amylase enzymes</strong></li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">These enzymes create less fermentable sugars (dextrins)</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">These sugars add body, mouthfeel, and sweetness to the final beer</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">Works faster than beta-amylase</li>
</ul><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]"><strong>Why Not Just One Temperature?</strong></p><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">By using multiple temperature steps, you control the balance between:</p><ul class="[li_&amp;]:mb-0 [li_&amp;]:mt-1.5 [li_&amp;]:gap-1.5 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ul]:pb-1 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ol]:pb-1 list-disc flex flex-col gap-2 pl-8 mb-3">
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>Fermentable sugars</strong> (higher alcohol, drier finish) vs <strong>non-fermentable sugars</strong> (body, sweetness, mouthfeel)</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">This gives you precise control over the final beer's character</li>
</ul><p>

</p><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">For a Märzen, this step mash creates a beer with good body and mouthfeel while still having enough fermentable sugars for a proper alcohol content - exactly what the style demands!</p><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">Stirring the mash is vital during the mashing!</p><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]"><!--IMAGE: CO2 regulator and pressure gauges (IMG_9980.jpeg)--><span style="-webkit-text-stroke-width: 0px; <p><strong>Hops:</strong> - Tettnanger 4.4% Vorderwürze: 104.35 g - Magnum/Taurus 13 - nach Würzebruch: 39.13 g</p><h3 style="text-align: left;">About the Hops</h3><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]"><strong>Tettnanger 4.4% - Vorderwürze: 104.35 g</strong></p><ul class="[li_&amp;]:mb-0 [li_&amp;]:mt-1.5 [li_&amp;]:gap-1.5 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ul]:pb-1 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ol]:pb-1 list-disc flex flex-col gap-2 pl-8 mb-3">
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>Tettnanger</strong> is a traditional German noble hop variety - mild, floral, and spicy</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>4.4%</strong> is the alpha acid content (relatively low = less bitterness)</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>Vorderwürze</strong> means "first wort" - these hops are added to the wort before or at the very start of the boil</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">First wort hopping creates a smoother, more integrated bitterness</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">The large quantity (104g) provides the base bitterness and aroma for the Märzen</li>
</ul><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]"><strong>Magnum/Taurus 13 - nach Würzebruch: 39.13 g</strong></p><ul class="[li_&amp;]:mb-0 [li_&amp;]:mt-1.5 [li_&amp;]:gap-1.5 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ul]:pb-1 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ol]:pb-1 list-disc flex flex-col gap-2 pl-8 mb-3" style="text-align: left;">
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>Magnum</strong> is a high alpha acid bittering hop</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>13%</strong> alpha acid content (high = strong bittering power)</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>nach Würzebruch</strong> means "after the hot break", which is a stage of the cooking process</li>
<ul><li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">Added part way through the boil, after the hot break, for clean, efficient bittering</li></ul>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">Smaller quantity needed due to higher alpha acid content</li>
</ul><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]"><strong>Why Different Hops?</strong>
Using two hop varieties gives you both efficient bittering (Magnum) and the traditional noble character (Tettnanger) that defines a Märzen. The sparge water:</p><ul class="[li_&amp;]:mb-0 [li_&amp;]:mt-1.5 [li_&amp;]:gap-1.5 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ul]:pb-1 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ol]:pb-1 list-disc flex flex-col gap-2 pl-8 mb-3">
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>Rinses the remaining sugars</strong> from the grain bed</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>Brings the total wort volume up</strong> to the target (we started with 78 L, added 81 L = 159 L total, which boils down to about&nbsp; 120 L final volume)</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>78°C temperature</strong> stops enzyme activity and helps the sugars flow more easily without extracting harsh tannins from the grain husks</li>
</ul><p>

<p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">After collecting the wort from the lautering and sparging process, it's time for the main boil. The wort must reach boiling temperature (around 97°C) before the 90-minute timer starts.</p>
<p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]"><strong>Why Boil for 90 Minutes?</strong></p>
<p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">The boil serves several critical purposes:</p>
<ul class="[li_&amp;]:mb-0 [li_&amp;]:mt-1.5 [li_&amp;]:gap-1.5 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ul]:pb-1 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ol]:pb-1 list-disc flex flex-col gap-2 pl-8 mb-3">
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>Sterilizes the wort</strong> - kills any bacteria or wild yeasts</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>Concentrates the wort</strong> - evaporates excess water to hit the target volume (about 120L final)</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>Extracts bitterness from hops</strong> - the high temperature isomerizes the alpha acids in hops, creating the bitter flavor</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>Drives off unwanted volatile compounds</strong> - removes DMS (dimethyl sulfide) which can create off-flavors</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>Coagulates proteins</strong> - the "hot break" causes proteins to clump together and settle out, improving clarity</li>
</ul>
<p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]"><strong>The Hot Break</strong></p>
<p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">During the early stages of the boil, you'll see foam and protein matter rise to the surface - this is called the "hot break" (Würzebruch in German). This is when we added our Magnum hops for bittering.</p></div>

This saved a lot of water at least!</p><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]"><strong>Cooling the Wort</strong></p><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">Normally the hot wort needs rapid cooling from around 97°C after the boil, as yeast can only survive and work at much lower temperatures (typically 18-22°C for ale yeast). Rapid cooling is preferred for several reasons:</p><ul class="[li_&amp;]:mb-0 [li_&amp;]:mt-1.5 [li_&amp;]:gap-1.5 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ul]:pb-1 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ol]:pb-1 list-disc flex flex-col gap-2 pl-8 mb-3">
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>Prevents contamination</strong> - the danger zone for bacteria is between 30-50°C, so ideally we want to pass through this range as quickly as possible</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>Allows yeast to be pitched sooner</strong> - yeast will die if added to hot wort</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>Improves clarity</strong> - rapid cooling helps proteins coagulate and settle out (called "cold break")</li>
</ul><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">

</p><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">However, for this batch we opted for the slower overnight cooling method, which still produces good results with less equipment and water usage. We will add the yeast in the morning.</p><table align="center" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="tr-caption-container" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"><tbody><tr><td style="text-align: center;"><a href="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEjJOFCEcPZAn3i7TZFn1frQeO3zgk6L9dyamSmTOjpMMQqoLJQFj1ZC9J329dxO3yTgQ8jBTJ5PRh_sDnISJnS4a1Vc6huXmBcTRZqAKWayDdHCI5l7oNn1fJZCUsJqKLalgXjqQzMrtyAZ3ay5TKlS-r-O_dw51k6Mrehc4YhVWMBgnRUQuIOe/s2048/IMG_0023.JPEG" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"><img border="0" data-original-height="1536" data-original-width="2048" height="480" src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEjJOFCEcPZAn3i7TZFn1frQeO3zgk6L9dyamSmTOjpMMQqoLJQFj1ZC9J329dxO3yTgQ8jBTJ5PRh_sDnISJnS4a1Vc6huXmBcTRZqAKWayDdHCI5l7oNn1fJZCUsJqKLalgXjqQzMrtyAZ3ay5TKlS-r-O_dw51k6Mrehc4YhVWMBgnRUQuIOe/w640-h480/IMG_0023.JPEG" width="640" /></a></td></tr><tr><td class="tr-caption" style="text-align: center;">For the transfer to fermentation vessel we also filter one last time with a sterilised filter sock to improve clarity of the final product</td></tr></tbody></table><h3 style="text-align: left;"><strong>Fermentation</strong></h3><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">Once in the fermentation vessel, yeast is added (pitched) and the vessel is sealed with an airlock. The yeast will now convert the fermentable sugars into alcohol and CO₂ over the next few weeks. Our Märzen will ferment for approximately 6-10 weeks before it's ready for the next stage.</p><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">When the yeast has stopped working, as determined by taking hydrometer readings over several days showing no change in specific gravity, the beer is ready for kegging. At this point, most of the fermentable sugars have been converted to alcohol, and the yeast has settled to the bottom of the vessel.</p><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">The finished beer is then transferred to a series of 50 L Fässer (kegs) and carbonated with CO₂. This is a point of difference from past brautag where bottle conditioning was used. The keg approach gives us more control over carbonation levels and makes serving much easier - no bottles to clean, fill, cap, or store!</p><p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">
